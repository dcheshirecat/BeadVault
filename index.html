📖 How to Read Beading Patterns

Beading patterns can look intimidating at first, but once you understand the symbols and conventions, they become your roadmap to creating beautiful beadwork. This guide covers both wire and Fireline beading patterns.

Understanding Pattern Diagrams

Most beading patterns use visual diagrams where:

  • Circles or ovals represent individual beads
  • Lines show the thread path through beads
  • Colors indicate different bead colors (check the color key!)
  • Numbers indicate the order to add beads or rows
  • Arrows show the direction of your needle/thread

Fireline & Thread Patterns

Fireline (braided fishing line) is popular for bead weaving because it's strong and has minimal stretch.

Key Conventions:
  • Start beads are often shown in a different color or marked with ⭐
  • Dotted lines show thread that passes through beads multiple times
  • UP beads vs DOWN beads indicate which beads your needle exits

Wire Beading Patterns

Wire beading patterns focus on wire gauge, wrapping techniques, and structural support:

  • Wire gauge numbers: Higher numbers = thinner wire (24g is thicker than 28g)
  • Wire types: Copper, sterling silver, gold-filled, or craft wire
  • Wrapping notation: Patterns may indicate number of wraps (3x, 5x)
  • Tool requirements: Round-nose pliers, flat-nose pliers, wire cutters

Common Abbreviations

Patterns use shorthand to save space:

  • DB - Delica bead (cylinder bead, size 11/0)
  • RB - Round bead
  • R# - Row number
  • St - Stitch
  • Rep - Repeat
  • PNT - Pass needle through
  • *...* - Section to repeat multiple times

Reading Stitch-Specific Patterns

Peyote Stitch

Peyote patterns show beads offset in a brick-like pattern. Odd-count peyote has a turn-around at row ends; even-count is simpler. Count beads in the first two rows to determine if it's even or odd.

Right Angle Weave (RAW)

RAW patterns show beads in square formations. Each "unit" has 4 beads (top, right, bottom, left). The needle typically exits from a different bead in each unit.

Brick Stitch

Brick stitch patterns look similar to peyote but the thread path is different. You stitch under the thread bridge between beads from the previous row.

Pro Tip: Always read through the ENTIRE pattern before starting. Note the materials needed, the starting technique, and any special increases/decreases. Make a sample swatch first!

Material Lists

Pattern material lists specify:

  • Bead sizes: 11/0, 8/0, 6/0 (smaller number = larger bead)
  • Bead types: Seed beads, Delicas, crystals, pearls
  • Quantities: Usually in grams or number of beads
  • Thread: Type (Fireline, Nymo, etc.) and weight (6lb, 8lb)
  • Findings: Clasps, jump rings, ear wires

Troubleshooting Pattern Reading

  • Lost your place? Use a row counter or mark completed rows with a highlighter
  • Confused by the diagram? Check if there's a written instruction version
  • Colors unclear? Reference the bead color key, often at the top or bottom
  • Tension issues? Your beadwork should match the diagram's structure
